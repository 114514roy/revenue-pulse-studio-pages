The trap: AI made the app possible, not sellable

AI programming can compress development time, but it does not automatically create buyer urgency, trust, proof, pricing, or a clear App Store search wedge. A customer is not choosing between your app and Codex. They are choosing between ignoring the problem, using a messy workaround, buying a clearer alternative, or trusting your app to solve one painful job.

The first question is not "what feature should I add?" It is "which buying step is failing?"

Five failure modes

1. Exposure failure

The App Store page is not being found. This usually means weak keywords, no obvious category wedge, no competitor-adjacent phrasing, and no external page that says the same thing in searchable language.

2. Conversion failure

People can see the page, but the first screenshots do not show who it is for, what pain it removes, what output it creates, or why it is safer than doing nothing.

3. Paid-boundary failure

The premium feature is a feature, not a result. Buyers pay for a claim packet, export, report, deadline, audit trail, proof bundle, or saved decision. They rarely pay because a menu says "Pro".

4. Trust failure

The app looks like a generated side project: generic copy, unclear support, vague privacy, no sample output, and no concrete workflow. A paid app must feel calm, specific, and finished.

5. Evidence failure

You are counting the wrong thing. A public page returning HTTP 200 is not a sale. A download is not paid demand. A checkout click is interest, not income. Confirmed income needs payment-platform order readback.

A 20-minute diagnosis

  1. Write the exact buyer and the moment they would pay.
  2. Name the concrete output they get after paying.
  3. Rewrite the first screenshot as buyer, pain, output, proof.
  4. List the search phrases a desperate buyer would actually type.
  5. Check whether the page, checkout, and order readback are separate metrics.

If those five lines are fuzzy, building another screen will probably not fix sales.
